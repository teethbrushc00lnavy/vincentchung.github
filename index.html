Can Virtual Reality Enhance Scope Sensitivity? Experimental Evidence from the Amazon Rainforest

Vincent Chung

Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment Working Paper 433

This study compares the efficacy of using immersive virtual reality (VR) and high-quality video presentations to elicit ‘willingness to pay’ (WTP) for conservation activities in the Amazon Rainforest. It examines whether VR elicits valuations of conservation benefits that are sensitive to the scope or scale of conservation (‘scope-sensitive valuations’), and whether any benefits justify its higher cost. It also explores variations within responses to VR to identify experiential factors, such as feeling present in the environment or feeling physically uncomfortable, that moderate scope sensitivity. The study finds that high-quality video presentations generate scope-sensitive willingness to pay (WTP) for Amazon Rainforest conservation as effectively as immersive VR, while offering greater accessibility and lower cost than VR.

Can Virtual Reality Reduce Hypothetical Bias? Experimental Evidence from the Amazon Rainforest

Vincent Chung

Working Paper

This study finds that immersive virtual reality (VR) does not reduce hypothetical bias in contingent valuation (CV). Using a pre-registered 2×2 randomised experiment (N=246), the study compared hypothetical and real donations to Amazon rainforest conservation programmes under VR and high-quality video presentations. Both formats generated substantial hypothetical bias, with statistically indistinguishable calibration factors of 5.62 for VR and 5.13 for video. Exploratory analyses reveal that within VR, hypothetical donations increase with emotional engagement, perceived realism, and conservation scope, while real donations remain stable, indicating an asymmetric response to VR’s experiential features. These results indicate that while VR enhances experiential realism, this alone is insufficient to mitigate hypothetical bias. Moreover, VR’s affective features are associated with greater overstatement among participants reporting high emotional engagement or perceived realism. Researchers using VR for environmental valuation should consider additional bias-mitigation strategies beyond immersive presentation.

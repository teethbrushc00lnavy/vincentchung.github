Can Virtual Reality Enhance Scope Sensitivity? Experimental Evidence from the Amazon Rainforest

Vincent Chung

Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment, Working Paper 433 (2025)

This study compares the efficacy of using immersive virtual reality (VR) and high-quality video presentations to elicit 'willingness to pay' (WTP) for conservation activities in the Amazon Rainforest. It examines whether VR elicits valuations of conservation benefits that are sensitive to the scope or scale of conservation ('scope-sensitive valuations'), and whether any benefits justify its higher cost. It also explores variations within responses to VR to identify experiential factors, such as feeling present in the environment or feeling physically uncomfortable, that moderate scope sensitivity. The study finds that high-quality video presentations generate scope-sensitive willingness to pay (WTP) for Amazon Rainforest conservation as effectively as immersive VR, while offering greater accessibility and lower cost than VR.

Can Virtual Reality Reduce Hypothetical Bias? Experimental Evidence from the Amazon Rainforest

Vincent Chung

Working Paper. Status: Draft submitted

This study finds that immersive virtual reality (VR) does not reduce hypothetical bias in contingent valuation (CV). Using a pre-registered 2×2 randomised experiment (N=246), the study compared hypothetical and real donations to Amazon rainforest conservation programmes under VR and high-quality video presentations. Both formats generated substantial hypothetical bias, with statistically indistinguishable calibration factors of 5.62 for VR and 5.13 for video. Exploratory analyses reveal that within VR, hypothetical donations increase with emotional engagement, perceived realism, and conservation scope, while real donations remain stable, indicating an asymmetric response to VR's experiential features. These results indicate that while VR enhances experiential realism, this alone is insufficient to mitigate hypothetical bias. Moreover, VR's affective features are associated with greater overstatement among participants reporting high emotional engagement or perceived realism. Researchers using VR for environmental valuation should consider additional bias-mitigation strategies beyond immersive presentation.

Valuing Skin Sensitisation: A multi-country stated preference approach

Davide Conte, Chiara Sotis, Giles Atkinson, Vincent Chung, Damien Dussaux, Stavros Georgiou and Susana Mourato

Working Paper. Status: Draft submitted

Exposure to chemicals can cause skin sensitisation, leading to allergic contact dermatitis and other dermatological conditions. This has potential to significantly impact on people's quality of life via physical discomfort, psychological distress, and limitations on daily activities. This study contributes to the OECD's SWACHE series – Surveys to elicit Willingness to pay to Avoid Chemicals related negative Health Effects – to strengthen the foundations of cost-benefit analyses of the management and regulation of chemicals, including those chemicals implicated in skin sensitisation. Specifically, the current study presents results from a large-scale stated preference survey estimating the willingness-to-pay (WTP) to avoid skin sensitisation. The survey primarily used a discrete choice experiment (DCE) for this purpose, enabling an assessment of the multi-dimensional character of skin sensitisation. These dimensions, or attributes, included frequency, severity as well as visibility of affected areas of the body. Different versions of the survey were designed depending on whether participants indicate they are already sensitised or not given that, for the latter group, the risk of becoming sensitised is an important dimension. The SWACHE skin sensitisation surveys were implemented across 9 countries: Canada, Denmark, Netherlands, Japan, Poland, Spain, Switzerland, United Kingdom, and the United States of America.

Economic Appraisal of Sea Turtle Conservation in Costa Rica: A Contingent Valuation Approach

Vincent Chung

Working Paper.

This study uses the contingent valuation method to elicit the economic value associated with sea turtle conservation in light of sea level rise, using both U.S. and Costa Rica population. For the U.S. population, mean WTP for a Florida- and Costa Rica-based turtle conservation program is US$17.15 and US$14.25 respectively. Monthly mean WTP for Costa Ricans for a Costa Rica-based turtle conservation program is CR₡5973.41 ≈ US$9.00. The results demonstrate the presence of distance effect, which potentially explains the difference in reported WTP values for local and overseas turtle conservation program in the U.S. population context.

Willingness-to-Pay to Ban Octocrylene

Chiara Sotis, Davide Contu, Vincent Chung, Giles Atkinson, and Susana Mourato

Work-in-progress.

In this study, we examine public preferences for an EU/ EEA-wide restriction – currently being considered under European Union Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals (EU REACH) – in the use of octocrylene in cosmetic products. Used in these products, this substance acts as an ultraviolet (UV) filter, UV absorber and light stabiliser. Whilst it is used across a range of cosmetics, sunscreens and facial care products account for most use in terms of volume. Specifically, we use a stated preference study to estimate people's willingness to pay (WTP) for restricting use of octocrylene in these products. To put this in context, this is a WTP estimate of the benefits, for instance, one element of the regulatory outcome that might arise if the restriction was to be imposed. As such, this monetary value can be compared with estimates of the regulatory costs in a cost-benefit analysis (CBA) of the restriction proposal.
